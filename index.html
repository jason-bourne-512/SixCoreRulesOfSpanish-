6 Core Rules of Spanish

Spanish gives you more flexibility than English — but there are still patterns to follow. Master these six and you'll sound natural fast.

1

Every verb changes based on who's speaking

In English, most verbs stay the same for most pronouns. In Spanish, every grammatical person gets its own ending — in every tense.

Spanish
English
yo cocinoI cook
I cooksame
tú cocinasyou cook
you cooksame
él/ella cocinahe/she cooks
he cooksadds -s
nosotros cocinamoswe cook
we cooksame
ellos cocinanthey cook
they cooksame
2

Subject pronouns are optional

Because the verb ending already tells you who's doing the action, you can drop the subject pronoun entirely. This is called a "pro-drop" language.

Spanish
Empiezo a las 8pm.
English (you must say "I")
I start at 8 pm.
Spanish
¿Terminas tan tarde?
English
Do you finish that late?
When to include the pronoun: Use it for emphasis ("YO lo hice, no tú") or to avoid ambiguity when the verb form is shared (él/ella/usted all share the same ending).
3

Pronouns go before the verb — usually

When you replace a noun with a pronoun (him, her, it, me), that pronoun moves in front of the verb. This is the opposite of English.

Quiero un coche. → I want a car.
Lo quiero. → I want it. (pronoun moves before verb)

But with imperative, gerund, or infinitive forms, the pronoun attaches after the verb:

After imperative
Siéntese, por favor.
English
Sit down, please.
After gerund
Estoy bañándome.
English
I'm taking a bath.
After infinitive
¿Quieres darme este cuaderno?
English
Do you want to give me this notebook?
4

The verb can go before the subject

The default order is Subject → Verb → Object, just like English. But Spanish lets you flip the verb and subject to shift emphasis.

Subject + Verb + Object — default, neutral
Neutral emphasis
Los invitados entraron a la reunión.
English
The guests entered the meeting.
Verb + Subject + Object — emphasizes the action
Verb-first (more dramatic)
Entraron los invitados a la reunión.
English (same meaning)
The guests entered the meeting.
5

Adjectives go after the noun

This is one of the most common mistakes English speakers make. In Spanish, the noun comes first, then the description.

PhraseSpanish ✓English order ✗
a red carun carro rojoun rojo carro
a black catun gato negroun negro gato
a delicious soupuna sopa deliciosauna deliciosa sopa
Exception: Some adjectives can go before the noun for stylistic or poetic effect — mi querida amiga (my dear friend) — but when in doubt, put it after.
6

Adverbs are flexible — but follow type-rules

Many adverbs can move around the sentence. Where they land depends on what they're modifying.

Modifying the whole sentence — can go almost anywhere
Juan come su sopa lentamente.
Lentamente, Juan come su sopa.
Juan lentamente come su sopa.
Juan slowly eats his soup — all three are correct.
Modifying an adjective or another adverb — goes before
Soy muy feliz.
I am very happy.
Adverbs of negation — always before the verb
Yo nunca salgo después de las 9.
I never go out past 9 at night.
Part two

Three Types of Sentences

Every sentence you build in Spanish is either a statement, a negation, or a question. Each has its own pattern.

Basic affirmative statement

The structure is the same as English: Subject + Verb + Object. You can also drop the subject (Rule 2) or flip the verb (Rule 4).

Subject + Verb + Object
Spanish
Yo como una manzana.
English
I eat an apple.
No subject needed
Siempre cocino para mi madre.
English
I always cook for my mother.

Add no before the verb

Making any sentence negative is simple: just place no directly before the verb. Nothing else changes.

Subject + No + Verb + Object
Affirmative → Negative
Yo no como una manzana.
English
I don't eat an apple.

Double negatives are correct in Spanish

Spanish allows (and requires!) two negative words in the same sentence. This is grammatically perfect — unlike in English.

Spanish ✓
No como ninguna manzana.
Literal English ✗
I don't eat no apple.
Spanish ✓
No me gusta nada.
English equivalent
I don't like anything.
Spanish ✓
No quiero nada de ti.
English equivalent
I don't want anything from you.
Key negative words: nada (nothing), nunca (never), nadie (nobody), ninguno/a (none). When paired with no, they go after the verb.

Three ways to ask a question

Spanish gives you three easy methods. All require the upside-down question mark ¿ at the start.

1

Add question marks + rising intonation

Take any statement, wrap it in ¿ ?, and raise your voice at the end. That's it.

Spanish
¿Yo como una manzana?
English
Do I eat an apple?
2

Swap verb and subject

Put the verb before the subject. Natural and very common in spoken Spanish.

Spanish
¿Escribe Juan?
English
Does Juan write?
3

Add a question tag

End your statement with a comma and ¿verdad?, ¿no?, or ¿sí? — like English "right?" or "isn't it?"

All three mean the same thing
Juan escribe libros, ¿verdad?
Juan escribe libros, ¿no?
Juan escribe libros, ¿sí?
English equivalent
Juan writes books, doesn't he?
